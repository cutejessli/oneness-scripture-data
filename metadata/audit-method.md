# Complete Audit Method

This project should not be treated as a keyword-replacement Bible rewrite. Each finished chapter must be translated and reviewed from the source-language level before it is marked complete.

## Status Levels

- `draft`: readable restored translation exists, but has not been fully audited.
- `audited`: every verse has been reviewed against source-language wording, grammar, and context.
- `source-critical`: manuscript and textual-variant issues have also been reviewed where relevant.

## Required Work for an Audited Chapter

For every verse:

1. Review the source-language text.
2. Identify key Hebrew, Aramaic, or Greek words.
3. Check grammar that affects translation: number, gender, verb stem, tense/aspect, article, preposition, and subject/object relationships.
4. Translate the whole verse, not only selected keywords.
5. Mark interpretive choices clearly.
6. Keep the main text readable.
7. Put explanations in notes rather than forcing them into the verse.
8. Preserve book, chapter, and verse structure.
9. Keep the familiar public-domain comparison text separate.
10. Add glossary links for terms that recur or carry theological weight.

## Source Priorities

### Hebrew Bible

Primary base text: Masoretic Hebrew, preferably represented by the Westminster Leningrad Codex or another reliable edition of the Masoretic tradition.

Audit comparisons where relevant:

- Dead Sea Scrolls
- Septuagint
- Samaritan Pentateuch for Torah passages
- Targums
- Vulgate
- major modern scholarly notes when available

### New Testament

Primary base text: a critical Greek text suitable for open use, such as SBLGNT where licensing permits.

Audit comparisons where relevant:

- major Greek manuscript traditions
- Sinaiticus and Vaticanus where relevant
- early versions
- major modern scholarly notes when available

## Translation Rules

1. Do not automatically preserve inherited church vocabulary.
2. Do not automatically replace words mechanically.
3. Translate contextually.
4. When a word has multiple possible meanings, choose the reading that best fits the verse and record the decision in notes.
5. Avoid overclaiming certainty when the source is ambiguous.
6. Do not soften a word only because it is uncomfortable. If the source is strong, preserve that strength and explain it.
7. Do not exaggerate a word into later doctrine. Satan, hell, sin, flesh, spirit, kingdom, and apocalypse must be handled separately by actual source terms.

## Chapter Completion Checklist

A chapter can be marked `audited` only when it includes:

- all verses
- restored translation
- familiar public-domain comparison
- key term links
- notes for major translation decisions
- an audit status field
- a reviewer note or timestamp
- no known malformed JSON

## Important Project Principle

The goal is not to make the Bible say what we want. The goal is to recover the broadest honest meaning the source language allows, identify where later translation narrowed or distorted it, and present a clean readable restoration with notes where needed.
